## 5 Things to Check Before Hiring

**1. Do they offer a free site visit?**
A good carpenter or furniture contractor will come to your home, measure the actual space, and give an honest estimate — for free. Measurements cannot be done over the phone. Anyone who quotes without visiting first is guessing, and that leads to surprises later. A free site visit also gives you the chance to judge whether you trust the person before handing over any money.

**2. Do they give a written warranty?**
Verbal promises mean nothing once the job is done. Ask specifically: "Will I get a written warranty on the workmanship and hardware?" A contractor who offers one in writing is confident in their work. A 1-year written warranty on furniture is the minimum standard you should expect.

**3. Can they show you past work in Indore?**
Ask for real photos of kitchens, wardrobes, and furniture they have built — not stock images from a catalogue. Better still, ask if they have any completed jobs nearby that you can look at. A local contractor with 30+ years of work in Indore should have no shortage of references.

**4. Do they handle the whole project?**
A furniture contractor is different from a basic carpenter. A contractor manages the full scope — material sourcing, fabrication, installation, hardware, and finishing. If you have to separately coordinate a painter, plumber, and supplier, that is not a contractor. Ask upfront: "Who sources the materials? Who installs it? Who do I call if something needs fixing?"

**5. Are they local and do they respond quickly?**
Someone who knows Indore homes — the typical flat sizes, the heat and humidity, the local timber suppliers, the standard kitchen layouts in Vijay Nagar versus Rau — will give better practical advice than a large company with a call centre. Local also means faster response for repairs and follow-up work.

## 5 Red Flags When Hiring a Carpenter in Indore

Watch out for these warning signs:

**1. No written warranty offered.** Any contractor unwilling to put their warranty commitment in writing is telling you something about how confident they are in their own work. Walk away.

**2. Vague pricing.** "It depends" is a valid first answer — real pricing requires a site visit. But if pricing stays vague *after* a site visit, that is a red flag. You should get a clear, itemised estimate before any work begins.

**3. No photos of real past work.** If the only images you see are catalogue photos or stock images, you cannot judge their actual workmanship. Always ask for photos from real projects they have completed in Indore.

**4. They subcontract the actual work.** Some people advertise as carpenters but subcontract the physical work to whoever is available. This means inconsistent quality and no accountability. Ask directly: "Is this work done by your own team or subcontracted?"

**5. No local address or verifiable local presence.** A real local business has a verifiable presence — neighbours who know them, past clients in the area, a physical location you could visit. An anonymous listing on an app with no local footprint is a much higher risk.

## Questions to Ask Before You Hire

Before committing to any carpenter or contractor in Indore, ask these specific questions:

- **"Can you show me a completed modular kitchen or wardrobe nearby?"** A contractor confident in their work will welcome this. It also lets you judge quality in person.
- **"What plywood grade do you use for the carcass?"** The correct answer is BWP (Boiling Waterproof) or BWR (Boiling Water Resistant) for any furniture that will be exposed to Indore's humidity and heat. MDF is not an appropriate carcass material.
- **"Is your warranty in writing?"** Ask for this specifically, not just a verbal confirmation.
- **"Who actually does the work — your own team or a subcontractor?"** Own-team work is consistently better quality.
- **"What hardware brand do you use for hinges and channels?"** Good contractors use Hettich, Hafele, or equivalent quality brands. Low-quality local hardware fails within 2 to 3 years.

## Understanding Materials — What to Ask For

Most homeowners do not know what to ask for when it comes to furniture materials. Contractors know this, and some use it to cut corners. Here is the minimum you should know:

**BWP vs MDF for the carcass:**
BWP (Boiling Waterproof) or BWR (Boiling Water Resistant) plywood is the right material for the main structure (carcass) of any kitchen, wardrobe, or storage unit. It handles Indore's humidity, resists swelling, and lasts 15 to 20 years. MDF (Medium Density Fibreboard) swells when exposed to moisture and is not appropriate as a structural carcass material in a kitchen. MDF can be used for decorative panels and painted surfaces, but not the core structure.

**Hettich vs local hardware:**
The hinges and drawer channels are the parts you interact with every day. Quality hardware from Hettich (or Hafele, Blum, or equivalent) operates smoothly, self-closes softly, and does not loosen over years of use. Cheap local hardware feels fine initially but loosens, squeaks, and fails within 2 to 3 years. The cost difference per kitchen is relatively small — ask which brand is being used before agreeing.

**Laminate vs acrylic shutters:**
For kitchen and wardrobe shutters, laminate is durable and comes in hundreds of finishes. Acrylic (high-gloss) looks premium but shows fingerprints and is harder to repair if scratched. Both are valid choices — what matters is the quality of the base material (plywood, not particle board) and the thickness and brand of the laminate.

## Why a Local Specialist Beats an Aggregator App

Apps like Urban Company and JustDial are useful for finding people, but they have structural problems for furniture and carpentry work:

**Speed.** A local carpenter you know can come the same day for a repair. An app assigns whoever is available, which can take days.

**Accountability.** If something goes wrong with a local contractor's work, you know where to find them. With an app platform, accountability is diffuse — the platform, the assigned contractor, and the warranty process all point at each other.

**Real warranty.** Aggregator platforms offer platform-level guarantees that are hard to enforce for complex carpentry work. A local contractor who gives you a written warranty is personally accountable for it.

**No hidden platform fees.** Contractors who work through apps pay a significant commission to the platform. This either reduces what they can afford to spend on materials, or inflates your price, or both.

**They know Indore homes.** A contractor who has spent 30 years working in Indore knows the standard flat layouts, the local material suppliers, the humidity issues, and what actually works. A nationally-managed platform sends whoever is available — often without that specific local knowledge.
