One of the first questions people ask when considering custom furniture is: *"How much will it actually cost?"* The honest answer is — it depends. But here's what it depends on, so you can plan without surprises.

## What Actually Drives the Price

**1. Material choice**
This is the biggest cost variable. The same wardrobe in commercial-grade plywood costs very differently than one in BWP (Boiling Waterproof) marine ply. Both are valid choices — it depends on your location in the house and budget.

**2. Size and complexity**
A straight modular kitchen costs less than a U-shaped one. A plain wardrobe costs less than one with a mirror panel, soft-close channels, and built-in lighting. Every design decision has a cost attached.

**3. Hardware quality**
Soft-close hinges, channels, handles, and locks are the parts of furniture you interact with every single day. Cheap hardware fails in 2–3 years. Good hardware lasts 10+ years. The price gap between average and good hardware is smaller than you'd expect — but the difference in experience is huge.

**4. Finishing and laminate**
High-gloss laminates cost more than matte. Textured finishes cost more than plain. PVC edging quality matters too.

## Realistic Budget Ranges for Indore

These are approximate starting ranges for standard work:

- **Modular kitchen (straight, 8–10 ft):** ₹80,000 – ₹1,50,000+
- **L-shaped modular kitchen:** ₹1,20,000 – ₹2,50,000+
- **Sliding wardrobe (6 ft wide):** ₹35,000 – ₹80,000+
- **TV unit (wall-mounted, 6 ft):** ₹20,000 – ₹50,000+
- **Full bedroom set:** ₹90,000 – ₹2,00,000+

Final prices vary significantly based on the factors above. Always get a site visit and written estimate before committing.

## Two Ways to Work with a Furniture Contractor

**With Material** — the contractor sources everything. You get a single quoted price, one point of accountability, and no running around to hardware shops. Slightly higher total cost, but zero hassle.

**Percentage Basis** — you arrange the material, the contractor provides labour and execution. Lower cost, but you carry the responsibility of sourcing.

Neither is better — it depends on your time, budget, and preference.
